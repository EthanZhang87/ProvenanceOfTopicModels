Hidden headlamps also commonly known as pop up headlamps pop up headlights flip eye headlamps or hideaway headlights are a form of automotive lighting and an automotive styling feature that conceals an automobile s headlamps when they are not in use br Depending on the design the headlamps may be mounted in a housing that rotates so as to sit flush with the front end as on the Lamborghini Miura or Porsche may retract into the hood and or fenders as on the Chevrolet Corvette or may be concealed behind retractable or rotating grille panels as on the Dodge Charger Mercury Cyclone or the Buick Riviera br br br History br Hidden headlamps first appeared on the Cord in November at the New York Auto Show and shortly after on a custom example of the Alfa Romeo C in In the Cord a pair of cranks on either side of the dashboard could be turned by hand to bring out the headlamps when needed Powered hidden headlamps first appeared on GM s concept car in the Buick Y Job and appeared on concept cars for several years after including the General Motors Le Sabre However power hidden headlamps would not appear on a production vehicle until with the Lotus Elan br The popularity of this feature has waxed and waned over time Hidden headlamps regained popularity in the mid to late s first in Europe but particularly in the US where aerodynamic headlamps were not permitted A relatively large variety of cars incorporated hidden headlamps in the s s s and up to the early s Subsequent legislation led to hidden headlamps falling increasingly out of favor br In the past manufacturers often used hidden headlamps to circumvent headlight height regulations in the United States For instance in Toyota exported their retractable headlight version of the AE known domestically as the Sprinter Trueno instead of the Corolla Levin as the former had a higher headlamp height enough to satisfy US regulations This prevented them from needing to raise the body height of the car which would have affected handling br br br Discontinuation br US laws now permit aerodynamic headlamps relative to which hidden headlamps represent added cost weight and complexity as well as reliability concerns as cars age Internationalized ECE auto safety regulations have also recently incorporated pedestrian protection provisions restricting protuberances from car bodies making it more difficult and expensive to design compliant pop up headlamps br The last time pop up headlamps appeared on a volume production car was in when both the Lotus Esprit and Chevrolet Corvette C ended production Development of both projector beam headlamps such as those on the Nissan ZX Z and more efficient bright LED headlamps has in practice eliminated the need for hidden headlamps altogether br Despite new cars no longer
